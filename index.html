Lose 0.5–0.7 kg/week Retain Muscle & Strength 180–200g Protein · 2,100–2,300 kcal 8,000–10,000 Steps 8h Sleep Fix Desk Posture

Recovery & Conditioning
Cardio Protocol — LISS Only During Cut
WHY LISS, NOT HIIT: You're training 4 days/week at 18–20 working sets per session in a caloric deficit. Recovery capacity is already reduced. HIIT draws from the same recovery pool as lifting — it creates systemic fatigue, elevates cortisol, depletes glycogen, and taxes the CNS. The caloric burn difference is ~50–80 kcal per session — just eat slightly less instead. LISS burns calories without adding recovery debt.
W Wednesday — Primary Cardio Day ▼
Best cardio day of the week. No training before or after — maximum separation from resistance sessions. Full recovery from Mon/Tue block, full recovery into Thu/Fri block.
Option A — Walk
30–45 minutes brisk walk at conversational pace. Heart rate 50–65% of max. Evening when it cools down is ideal. Burns ~200–270 kcal at 91 kg.
Option B — Swim
20–30 minutes at moderate pace. Keep it easy — swimming taxes lats, shoulders, and triceps more than people assume. Don't turn it into a workout. Pick either walk or swim, not both.
+ Mobility Work
10–15 minutes of stretching. Focus on hip flexor stretches, thoracic spine rotations, and pigeon pose. This directly improves squat depth and overhead press position for Thursday/Friday.
S Saturday — Second Cardio Day ▼
Second-best cardio day. Maximum separation from training. Same options as Wednesday. If you swim, avoid hammering shoulders the day before Upper A on Monday.
Walk or Swim
30–40 minutes walk or 20–30 minutes moderate swim. Same rules as Wednesday. If you swam Wednesday, walk Saturday (or vice versa) to avoid repetitive shoulder stress before Monday's upper session.
+ Training Days — Bonus (Not Required) ▼
Petré et al. 2021: spacing cardio at least 6–8 hours from resistance training minimizes interference. Post-workout cardio can impair mTOR signaling, which is already suppressed by the deficit (Areta et al. 2014). Separate sessions are better than post-workout if you can manage it.
If you can fit it in
15–20 minute walk at any point during the day, separate from your gym session. Morning, lunch break, evening — doesn't matter. If some days you can't, skip it. The resistance training is the priority. This is a bonus, not a requirement.
☀ Sunday — Full Rest ▼
Genuine rest. A short walk is fine if you feel like it, but this is the day before a training week starts. Sleep well, eat your protein, recover. Don't add stress.
Weekly Cardio Target: 2 dedicated LISS sessions (Wed + Sat) · 30–40 min each · ~500–700 kcal/week additional expenditure · Zero recovery interference

Research: Schoenfeld 2014 — no fat-loss advantage to fasted cardio · Wilson 2012 — LISS minimizes interference · Helms — avoid HIIT during deficit for natural trainees · Henselmans — LISS preserves muscle better than HIIT in caloric restriction
Daily Target
Step Count
👟 8,000–10,000 Steps / Day ▼
Paluch et al. 2022 (JAMA Internal Medicine): 8,000 steps/day associated with significant reduction in all-cause mortality. Benefits plateau around 10,000–12,000. For someone in a deficit, steps are the easiest way to increase NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) without structured cardio.
How to hit it with a desk job
A typical office worker gets 3,000–4,000 steps passively. You need to deliberately add 4,000–6,000 more. That's roughly 30–45 minutes of total walking broken up throughout the day — not a single long walk.
Realistic breakdown
Morning: 10-min walk before work or after parking (~1,200 steps)
Lunch: 15-min walk after eating (~1,800 steps)
Evening: 15-min walk post-dinner (~1,800 steps)
Throughout day: Take calls standing/walking, use stairs, walk to refill water (~1,000 steps)
Training day steps
Your gym session itself adds ~2,000–3,000 steps. So on training days you need less deliberate walking. On rest days (Wed/Sun), you need to be more intentional since you lose that baseline.
💺 Beating the Desk — Sedentary Office Tips ▼
Prolonged sitting reduces insulin sensitivity, compresses hip flexors (already flagged as tight), inhibits glute activation, and drops NEAT significantly. Diaz et al. 2017 (Annals of Internal Medicine): sitting bouts >60 minutes continuously are associated with higher mortality regardless of total exercise.
The 30/30 rule
Every 30 minutes, stand for 30 seconds. Set a phone timer or use an app. Just standing and sitting back down breaks the continuous sitting pattern. This alone improves insulin sensitivity and blood glucose response (Dempsey et al. 2016).
Micro-movement breaks (every 60 min)
Standing hip flexor stretch — 20s/side. Your hip flexors are locked short for 8 hours. This directly affects squat depth and hip thrust lockout.
Thoracic extension over chair back — 10s. Counters the forward hunch. Improves overhead press position.
Glute squeeze — 10 reps standing. Reactivates glutes that go dormant from sitting (gluteal amnesia).
Walk to refill water — keeps you hydrated and adds steps.
Posture setup
Monitor at eye level. Elbows at 90°. Feet flat on floor. If you can, alternate between sitting and standing (even stacking books under your laptop counts). Your rear delt and face pull work in the gym is partially fixing posture problems that 8 hours of desk work creates daily — don't undo it by slouching all day.
Water intake
3–4 litres/day. Use a 1L bottle and refill it. This forces bathroom breaks which force you to stand and walk. Hydration also supports training performance and reduces false hunger signals during a deficit.
Nutrition Targets
Protein & Calorie Intake
P Protein — 180–200g / Day ▼
Helms et al. 2014 meta-analysis: resistance-trained athletes in a deficit need 2.3–3.1 g/kg of fat-free mass. At ~91 kg with an estimated 18–22% body fat, fat-free mass ≈ 71–75 kg → 163–232g range. Practically, 180–200g/day covers the sweet spot without needing precise body fat measurement.
Why this high
In a deficit, muscle protein breakdown increases. Higher protein intake offsets this by keeping muscle protein synthesis elevated. Phillips & Van Loon 2011: protein requirements increase as caloric deficit deepens. You're doing heavy compound work (145 kg squats, 120 kg RDLs) — the muscle damage from that training requires amino acids for repair. Skimping on protein in a deficit is the fastest way to lose muscle.
Distribution
4 meals × 40–50g protein each. Schoenfeld & Aragon 2018: distributing protein across 4+ feedings maximizes 24-hour MPS. Don't front-load it all at dinner.

Pre-training: 30–40g protein 1.5–2 hours before training
Post-training: 30–40g protein within 2 hours after training
Before bed: 30–40g casein-rich protein (curd/paneer/casein shake). Res et al. 2012: pre-sleep protein improves overnight MPS.
Practical sources at Indian pricing
Eggs (6g/egg) · Chicken breast (31g/100g) · Curd/Greek yoghurt (10g/100g) · Paneer (18g/100g) · Whey protein (24–25g/scoop) · Dal/lentils (9g/100g cooked) · Fish (20–25g/100g)

A realistic day: 4 eggs breakfast (24g) + chicken lunch (40g) + whey shake (50g from 2 scoops) + paneer/fish dinner (35g) + curd before bed (30g) = ~180g
C Calories — 2,100–2,300 kcal / Day ▼
Estimated TDEE at 91 kg, 4× resistance training, sedentary job + LISS ≈ 2,700–2,900 kcal. A 500–700 kcal/day deficit = ~0.45–0.65 kg/week loss. Research (Garthe et al. 2011): slower rate of loss (~0.7% BW/week) preserves significantly more muscle than aggressive cuts (~1.4% BW/week). At 91 kg, 0.7% = ~0.64 kg/week.
The math
BMR (Mifflin-St Jeor): ~1,850–1,950 kcal (estimated for ~91 kg male)
Activity multiplier: ×1.45–1.55 (sedentary job + 4× training + LISS)
Estimated TDEE: ~2,700–2,900 kcal
Target deficit: 500–700 kcal/day
Intake target: 2,100–2,300 kcal/day
Macro split
Protein: 180–200g → 720–800 kcal (priority — set this first)
Fat: 60–75g → 540–675 kcal (minimum ~0.7 g/kg for hormonal health — Helms)
Carbs: remaining → 150–200g → 600–800 kcal (fuels training, supports recovery)

Don't cut fats below 55g — testosterone production drops. Don't cut carbs below 130g — training performance tanks. Protein is non-negotiable. Adjust carbs and fats within these floors to hit your calorie target.
Tracking & adjusting
Start at 2,200 kcal. Weigh yourself daily, take the weekly average. If losing 0.5–0.7 kg/week → stay. If losing faster → add 100 kcal. If stalling for 2+ weeks → drop 100 kcal. Never go below 1,900 kcal at your bodyweight and training load — below that, recovery collapses and you start losing muscle regardless of protein intake.

Rate of loss: 0.5–0.7 kg/week (0.5–0.7% BW). Adjust calories every 2 weeks based on weekly weight average. These are estimates — your actual TDEE may differ. Track, measure, adjust.

Sleep restriction increases cortisol, reduces testosterone, increases ghrelin (hunger hormone), and impairs insulin sensitivity. All of these directly oppose your goals — you hold more fat, lose more muscle, feel hungrier, and recover slower. Dattilo et al. 2011: sleep deprivation reduces muscle protein synthesis by 18% even with adequate protein intake.

Carter et al. 2025 (American Journal of Clinical Nutrition): 2 eggs/day within a low-saturated-fat diet actually reduced LDL cholesterol compared to a high-saturated-fat egg-free diet. The key finding: saturated fat, not dietary cholesterol from eggs, is the primary driver of LDL levels. Framingham Offspring Study (2024): consuming 5+ eggs/week was not adversely associated with lipid outcomes in healthy adults.

Supplements
Creatine & Protein Timing
Cr Creatine Monohydrate — 5g / Day ▼
Kreider et al. 2017 (ISSN position stand): creatine monohydrate is the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass. Safe for long-term daily use. No loading phase needed — 5g/day reaches saturation in ~28 days.
When to take
Timing doesn't matter much — saturation is what counts (Kreider 2017). Antonio & Ciccone 2013 showed a slight advantage for post-workout, but the effect was small. Take it whenever you'll be consistent. Morning with water is simple. Don't overcomplicate this.
Practical
5g (one level teaspoon) of creatine monohydrate in water, every day including rest days. Buy the cheapest unflavoured creatine monohydrate — brand doesn't matter, it's all the same molecule. Expect 1–2 kg of water weight gain in the first 2 weeks — this is intramuscular water, not fat. Don't panic.
W Whey Protein — When & How Much ▼
Whey is a convenience tool, not magic. Jäger et al. 2017 (ISSN): no advantage to whey over whole food protein at equal doses. Use it when whole food is impractical (post-gym, before bed if you don't want curd).
Post-gym (9:00 AM): 1 scoop (25g protein) — fast-digesting, easy to consume during 9:30 call. Mix with water, not milk (saves ~100 kcal in a deficit).

Before bed (optional): If you can't do curd/paneer, casein protein is better than whey here — slower digestion sustains overnight MPS (Res et al. 2012). If you only have whey, mix it with curd to slow absorption.

Total from shakes: 1–2 scoops/day max. Get 70%+ of protein from real food. Shakes are for the gap, not the foundation.

Desk Health
Posture & Sitting Protocol
! What Crouching at a Desk Does to You ▼
Upper crossed syndrome (Janda): tight pecs + upper traps, weak deep neck flexors + lower traps. Lower crossed syndrome: tight hip flexors + erectors, weak glutes + abs. You're training the correct muscles (rear delts, face pulls, hip thrusts, core) — but 8+ hours of crouching daily will undo the training adaptations if you don't intervene during the workday.
Your specific damage pattern
Forward head posture — staring at monitor with chin jutting forward. Loads cervical spine at 5× normal (Hansraj 2014). Gives you neck pain and headaches.
Rounded shoulders — crouching forward tightens pecs and front delts, inhibits rear delts and mid traps. Your face pulls and rear delt work fights this, but 4 sets/week vs 40+ hours of crouching — crouching wins unless you intervene.
Hip flexor shortening — sitting for hours locks iliopsoas in shortened position. Directly reduces squat depth, reduces hip thrust lockout force, and causes anterior pelvic tilt (lower belly pooch even at low body fat).
Glute amnesia — prolonged sitting literally reduces neural drive to glutes (McGill 2007). Your hip thrusts fight this but desk work fights back.
The fix — fitted to your work schedule
Every 30 min: Stand for 30 seconds. Just stand up and sit back down. Set a phone timer. This alone improves insulin sensitivity (Dempsey et al. 2016).

Every 60 min (takes 90 seconds):
• Standing hip flexor stretch — 20s/side
• Chin tucks — 10 reps (fixes forward head posture)
• Squeeze shoulder blades together — hold 5s × 5 reps

During 1:30 PM break: 15-min walk after lunch + 5 min of the stretches above.
During 4:00 PM break: Stand, hip flexor stretch, 2-min walk. Even in 15 minutes you can reset.
Monitor & chair setup (WFH essentials)
Monitor height: Top of screen at eye level. If using a laptop, get a ₹500 laptop stand and external keyboard. This single change eliminates most forward head posture.
Chair: Feet flat on floor, knees at 90°, back against chair. If your chair is bad, a rolled towel behind your lower back is better than nothing.
20-20-20 rule for eyes: Every 20 min, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Reduces digital eye strain (Sheppard & Wolffsohn 2018).
Cognitive Fitness
Brain Training in the AI Era
🧠 The Problem — Cognitive Offloading ▼
Dergaa et al. 2024 (Frontiers in Psychology): AI chatbots promote "cognitive offloading" — delegating memory, analysis, and problem-solving to external tools. Risko & Gilbert 2016 (Trends in Cognitive Sciences): cognitive offloading reduces internal cognitive effort, and repeated offloading weakens the underlying skill — like a muscle you stop training. Gerlich 2025 (Societies): individuals who heavily use AI tools show reduced critical thinking scores and lower cognitive resilience.
You're not "getting stupid." What's happening is you're outsourcing cognitive effort to AI and social media algorithms, and the skills you're not using are decaying — exactly like muscle atrophy from not training. The research calls this "cognitive debt" (Kosmyna et al. 2025, MIT Media Lab): each act of outsourcing thinking accumulates a deficit in your own capacity.

The analogy is direct: your training programme exists because muscles atrophy in a deficit if you don't stimulate them. Your brain works the same way. AI removes the "load" from thinking → your thinking "muscles" atrophy → you feel stupider. The fix is the same: apply deliberate cognitive load.
Rx The Fix — Daily Cognitive Load Protocol ▼
Just as your body needs progressive overload, your brain needs "germane load" — cognitive effort directed at learning and problem-solving (Cognitive Load Theory, Sweller et al. 2011). The key is replacing passive consumption (scrolling, watching, asking AI) with active production (writing, solving, creating, recalling).
9:00–10:00 PM — Your Brain Training Window
Screens are off at 9:00 anyway for sleep. Use this hour for one of these (rotate through the week):
1. Deep Reading — 30 min (3× per week)
Physical book, not Kindle/phone. Fiction or non-fiction that requires sustained attention. Carr 2020 (The Shallows): deep reading activates regions for language, memory, and visual imagery simultaneously — the exact regions that atrophy with scrolling. Start with manga if that's easier — Vinland Saga requires more cognitive engagement than Instagram reels. Graduate to books. The goal is 30 minutes of uninterrupted focus on a single narrative.
2. Write by Hand — 15 min (2× per week)
Journal, problem analysis, or summarize something you learned that day — in a physical notebook. Mueller & Oppenheimer 2014 (Psychological Science): handwriting engages deeper cognitive processing than typing. Don't use AI to structure your thoughts first. The struggle of organizing messy ideas on paper IS the exercise. Write about a work problem, something you read, or just what happened that day.
3. Problem-Solve Without AI — 1× per week
When you hit a coding or data problem at work, try solving it yourself for 30 minutes before asking AI. Not for everything — just pick one problem per week where you work through the logic manually. Debug by reading the stack trace, trace the data flow, check the docs. The frustration you feel is the cognitive equivalent of muscle tension under load — it's the signal that growth is happening.
4. Learn Something New — Ongoing
Pick one skill that has nothing to do with your job. A language, chess, an instrument, mental math — anything with progressive difficulty. Lim et al. 2022 (Alzheimer's & Dementia): cognitively stimulating leisure activities are associated with reduced cognitive decline. The novelty is what matters — your brain adapts to routine work like your muscles adapt to the same exercises.
Social Media & YouTube — Set a Hard Boundary
Passive consumption (scrolling, autoplay) is the cognitive equivalent of lying on the couch instead of training. Set a daily time limit — 30 min max for entertainment content. Use Screen Time / Digital Wellbeing to enforce it. News and info YouTube is fine if you're actively learning, but be honest about the 80/20 split. If you're watching a 15-min video that could be a 2-min article, you're not learning — you're being entertained while feeling productive. That's the most dangerous form of cognitive offloading.
THE PARALLEL: You built a 4-day programme because muscles atrophy in a deficit without stimulus. Your brain operates on the same principle. AI removes cognitive load → cognitive skills atrophy → you feel stupider. The fix isn't avoiding AI — it's adding deliberate cognitive load the same way you add deliberate training load. Read, write, solve, create. 30–60 minutes per day. Non-negotiable, like protein intake.

100Cannot do another rep. Absolute failure.Rarely. Never heavy compounds.Never on squat/RDL/bench in deficit
KEY RULE: In a caloric deficit, compounds stay at RPE 7–8 (2–3 RIR). Only safe isolations go to RPE 9–10 on LAST set. Never grind heavy compounds to failure — drop a set instead.
